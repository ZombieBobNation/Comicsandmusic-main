The Question

This project started with a creative vision and a genuine concern.

How do I stay in control while using AI as a creative assistant?

The Fear

Once I put my idea into AI, would it feel like AI was stealing it? Would the work still feel like mine?

Related Questions

  • Can AI stay visually and narratively consistent?
  • When do I take over instead of letting AI keep generating?
  • Can AI-assisted work still feel intentional?

What I Discovered

Those concerns can be managed. AI needs structure, practice, and responsibility. It should not be used lightly.

The Reframe: AI is a tool like a wrench, guitar, or paintbrush. The human touch is putting feeling, emotion, comparison, taste, and direction into the work.

How the work actually happened: trials, corrections, and moments of discovery.

Key Discoveries

Black-and-White is Easier

B&W images were much easier to control. Color added complexity: armor colors, character identity, lighting, effects, backgrounds. This became one of the most important findings.

Save and Reuse Prompts

The best method for consistency was copying successful prompts into notes and reusing them. This prevented characters from drifting away from their original look.

Know When to Pivot

Sometimes it was better to readjust and move on than waste credits trying to fix one image forever. Not every output was worth fixing.

Prompting is Iterative

Prompting required repeating details, using references, rejecting bad outputs, and correcting mistakes. It took practice and patience, not just one perfect prompt.

AI Tools Used

ChatGPT

Organizer, prompt builder, and project guide. Helped decide what step happened next, shape lyrics, and define music style.

Firefly

Image generation for comic panels. Used to test visual style, explore character appearances, and compare black-and-white vs. color.

Figma Make

Comic panel layout and page organization. Helped move from individual images into a structured page-based format.

Suno

Music generation and production. Turned lyrics and style prompts into songs that could be published on streaming platforms.

NotebookLM

Process documentation. Helped explain the workflow and support how someone else could understand or repeat parts of it.

Research Approach

How I Researched

My research was mostly video-based: watching tutorials, testing tools, and constantly learning through practice. I do not like reading much, so I learned by doing.

Focus Areas

The most useful research focused on how to prompt for consistent imagery without losing style or character look, especially when showing multiple characters at once.

Verification Method

Most verification came from testing the tools myself. I questioned whether advice from videos actually worked by trying it inside my own project. If it worked, I reused it. If it failed, I adjusted.

Main Findings

But it is not automatic. It requires saving prompts, using references, and constant comparison against the original idea.

✓ Color Makes It Harder

Black-and-white images were much easier to control. Color added too many variables to juggle effectively.

✓ Pivoting is Smarter Than Persisting

Sometimes it was better to adjust and move on instead of wasting credits trying to fix one image forever.

✓ Research Shaped the Process

The research showed that AI is not just a button. It needs structure: saved prompts, references, feedback loops, and human judgment.

Authorship & Synthesis Statement

What I Made

I made a complete AI-assisted creative project that spans visual storytelling (comic) and audio production (music). Both outcomes are published and real. Both required constant human judgment, direction, and decision-making.

What is Strongest

The strongest element is that it proves human control is possible. I did not disappear into the AI. My choices matter. My judgment shaped the outcome. The work has intent and consistency because I guided it.

How AI Shaped the Outcome

AI accelerated the creation process and allowed me to explore possibilities faster. I could generate ten versions of a panel and choose the best one, instead of drawing all ten by hand. But AI did not make decisions for me. I made all the decisions. AI was the tool I used to make them.

What I Selected or Rejected

For the comic: I rejected panels that drifted too far from the character design. I rejected scenes that were too graphic. I rejected color versions in favor of black-and-white because I had more control. I selected 22+ black-and-white panels that best told the story.

For the music: I rejected versions that did not match the thrash metal style. I rejected songs that did not convey the intended emotion. I selected songs that felt intentional and aligned with the album theme.

What I Would Improve

I would invest more time in prompt engineering at the start. The faster I could write better prompts, the faster I could reach the final outcome. I would also explore more tools earlier, instead of sticking with one tool for too long when another might have been faster. Finally, I would plan the color palette earlier in the comic process instead of discovering halfway through that color made everything harder.

Learning & Reflection

What changed in my thinking, what clicked, and what comes next.

How My Thinking Changed

At the beginning of this project, I saw AI as something that maybe should not be used. I was worried it might steal my idea, make the work feel less like mine, or look bad to others. I felt oddly guilty for considering it.

By the end, I saw AI as a tool that can be used responsibly, but not casually. It takes practice, patience, and human judgment. AI is like a wrench, guitar, or paintbrush — it is just a tool. The person using it decides whether it is used well or poorly. I went from fear to understanding. That was the biggest shift.

The Click Moments

There were two major moments when things suddenly made sense.

The first click happened when I figured out how to make the comic work. Once I understood prompt saving, reference images, and the black-and-white trick, I was gone. I could generate panels. I could keep them consistent. The workflow became second nature. I went from struggling to generate one good panel to producing 22+ usable black-and-white panels.

The second click happened when I figured out how to make the music work. Once I understood how to shape lyrics in ChatGPT and then turn them into Suno prompts, I could generate songs. I could refine the style. I could publish them. Suddenly the music side made sense just like the comic side did.

Both times, once I figured out the process, the project accelerated. That taught me that AI tools need a learning curve. You do not just use them. You have to learn them. Once you do, they work.

What I Learned

I learned that AI-assisted work can still have intent and emotion if the person using it is actually steering it. It should not just be AI slop with no direction. It should have a reason, a goal, and human judgment behind it.

I also learned that prompting is not just about being specific one time. It is about being specific, testing, iterating, saving what works, and knowing when to pivot. Sometimes the problem is not that the prompt is wrong. Sometimes the problem is that the tool is wrong for that task, or the goal needs to change.

Finally, I learned that AI might have my kids beat at making me repeat myself. Getting AI to do what I want required saying things many times, many ways, until it understood. That taught me patience and precision.

What Comes Next

I want to expand the comic further. Now that I have the process down, I could add more panels, more chapters, and tell a longer story. I could explore color again with better prompt strategies. I could try other comic styles or genres.

I also want to experiment with mixing AI and other tools. Could I use AI-generated images as a base and then hand-edit or paint over them? Could I combine AI audio with other production techniques? Could I publish the project as an actual comic or music project outside of this class?

Most importantly, I want to keep treating AI as a tool that requires responsibility. Not every idea should use AI. Not every project needs AI. But when AI is the right tool for the right project, I now know it can work. My fear about ownership and control is gone. I know how to stay in control. That is the biggest win.

The project proved: AI can do a little or a lot, and the creator can still stay in control. The tradeoff is time and practice. But the work becomes real, intentional, and publishable. That is enough.
