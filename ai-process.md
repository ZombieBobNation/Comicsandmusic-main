# AI Process

## Project

**Comics and Music with AI**

This file explains how AI was used during the project. It is meant to help the coding agent understand the process side of the website.

The main idea of the project is not that AI made everything. The main idea is that AI was used as an assistant while the human creator stayed in control through prompting, selection, editing, rejection, and final decision-making.

## Main Inquiry

**How do I stay in control while using AI as a creative assistant?**

The project started with AI-assisted comic creation and later expanded into AI-assisted music production and publishing.

## AI Tools Used

### ChatGPT

Used for:

- organizing the project,
- deciding what step should happen next,
- building prompts,
- revising prompts,
- suggesting tools,
- interviewing the creator,
- shaping lyrics,
- helping define music style,
- and explaining the workflow.

ChatGPT acted like the project guide and prompt-building assistant.

### Firefly

Used for:

- generating comic imagery,
- testing visual style,
- creating comic panels,
- exploring character appearances,
- and testing black-and-white versus color outputs.

Firefly was mainly used for the image-making side of the comic process.

### Figma Make

Used for:

- comic panel layout,
- organizing comic pages,
- structuring the visual sequence,
- and helping the comic move from individual images into a page-based format.

### NotebookLM

Used for:

- explaining the process,
- helping make the workflow easier to follow,
- and supporting documentation of how someone else could understand or repeat parts of the process.

### Suno

Used for:

- turning lyrics, emotions, and music direction into songs,
- testing different music versions,
- finding a consistent sound,
- and producing AI-assisted music that could be published.

Suno was used for the music production side of the project.

## Comic Process

The comic process started with a scene idea:

Two twin brother knights confront each other. One brother confronts the other about deception. They are attacked, one kills the other, and that death summons an ancient unstoppable beast.

The process usually worked like this:

1. Start with the story scene or visual goal.
2. Use ChatGPT to decide what needed to happen next.
3. Build a prompt for the scene, character, or panel.
4. Generate imagery in Firefly.
5. Compare the result against the original idea.
6. If the image was wrong, revise the prompt.
7. Use reference images when needed.
8. Save successful prompts in notes.
9. Reuse saved prompts to keep characters consistent.
10. Place usable panels into comic pages with Figma Make.

## Music Process

The music process worked differently than the comic process.

The creator gave ChatGPT:

- lyrics,
- emotions,
- song ideas,
- story concepts,
- or answers from an interview.

ChatGPT helped shape those into:

- lyrics,
- song direction,
- genre descriptions,
- music style prompts,
- and release concepts.

Then the material was placed into Suno. Multiple versions were generated until one matched the intended feeling or style.

The music process usually worked like this:

1. Start with lyrics, emotion, or a song idea.
2. Use ChatGPT to build or refine the lyrics.
3. Use ChatGPT to create a music style prompt.
4. Place the lyrics and style prompt into Suno.
5. Generate multiple versions.
6. Listen and compare.
7. Keep the version that matched the idea best.
8. Continue refining until a consistent sound was found.
9. Use the final songs for release.

## Consistency Experiments

Consistency was one of the biggest challenges.

The comic side needed consistent:

- characters,
- armor,
- colors,
- lighting,
- backgrounds,
- monster design,
- and panel-to-panel style.

The most useful consistency method was saving successful prompts and reusing them.

Prompts were copied and pasted into notes so they could be reused later. This helped prevent characters from drifting away from their original look and style.

Reference images were also used when the AI misunderstood the direction.

## Black-and-White vs Color

Black-and-white comic generation was easier to control.

When the comic was black-and-white, the results were closer to what was needed. Once color was added, the process became harder because more variables had to be controlled.

Color added problems with:

- character identity,
- armor colors,
- glowing effects,
- background palettes,
- lighting,
- and consistency between images.

This became one of the most important discoveries in the project.

## Prompting Lessons

The project showed that prompting is not just about writing one perfect prompt.

Prompting required:

- repeating important details,
- saving useful prompts,
- reusing successful language,
- adding references,
- correcting mistakes,
- rejecting bad outputs,
- and knowing when to pivot.

Sometimes it was better to stop wasting credits on one image and move on to another part of the project.

## Failures and Pivots

Important failures and pivots included:

- AI outputs drifting away from the original character design,
- color making the comic harder to control,
- prompts needing repeated correction,
- some scenes needing to be adjusted because they were too violent or graphic,
- and learning that not every output was worth fixing.

Instead of accepting AI outputs as final, the creator had to constantly evaluate whether each result worked.

## Human Control

The project argues that AI can do a little or a lot, but the creator can still remain in control.

Human control showed up through:

- choosing the story,
- directing the visual style,
- deciding what prompts mattered,
- saving prompts,
- using references,
- rejecting wrong outputs,
- correcting colors,
- choosing final music versions,
- deciding on a consistent music style,
- and publishing selected work.

The human touch was not about adding fake mistakes to make the work look human. It was about putting emotion, purpose, comparison, taste, and direction into the process.

AI was treated as a tool, like a wrench, guitar, or paintbrush.

## Final Outcomes

- a sample comic with panels arranged on pages,
- three singles,
- and an album published on music platforms including Spotify, iTunes, and Apple Music.
