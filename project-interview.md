# Project Interview Notes

## Project Title

**Comics and Music with AI**

## One-Sentence Project Summary

This project is about using AI as an assistant for making comics and music, not letting AI do everything by itself. It started with comic-making and later expanded into AI-assisted music that could actually be released on platforms like Spotify, iTunes, and Apple Music.

## Starting Point

The project started with comic-making because I had a scene in my head that I really wanted to see visually.

The original idea was two knights who are twin brothers. One brother confronts the other about deception, they get attacked, and then one kills the other. That death summons an ancient, unstoppable, powerful beast.

At first, the project was not about AI in general. It came from a specific creative idea and the question of whether AI could help me turn that idea into something visual.

## Inquiry Question

The main question became:

**How do I stay in control while using AI as a creative assistant?**

Other questions that came up during the project were:

- Can AI help me turn an idea in my head into a comic?
- Can AI stay visually and narratively consistent?
- When do I take over instead of letting AI keep generating?
- How do I keep characters consistent, if that is even possible?
- Can AI-assisted creative work still feel like it belongs to me?

## Why This Question Mattered

Before using AI, one of my biggest fears was that once I opened my mind and put my idea into AI, it would feel like AI was stealing it.

That fear did not completely disappear, but the project helped me realize those concerns can be managed. AI still brings up questions about ownership, ethics, consistency, commercial use, and whether using it will look bad on a resume. There was also a weird guilt attached to using AI.

By the end, I understood AI more as a tool that needs control, practice, and responsibility. It should not be used lightly.

## Research and Sources

My research was mostly video-based because I do not like reading much. I learned by watching videos, testing tools, and constantly learning how to use AI through practice.

The most useful research focused on how to prompt for consistent imagery without losing the style or look of a character, especially when trying to show multiple characters at once.

The research was less about traditional articles and more about hands-on learning, tutorials, testing, and comparing what actually worked.

## What I Found

I found that AI consistency is possible, but it is not automatic.

It can take a lot of time to get the right image and then preserve the prompt that made it work. I had to copy and paste prompts into my notes and reuse them so characters did not drift away from their original style and look.

A big finding was that black-and-white comic images were easier to control. Once color was added, there were more things to juggle: armor colors, character identity, lighting, effects, backgrounds, and consistency across panels.

I also found that it is sometimes better to readjust and pivot instead of wasting credits trying to fix one image forever. If one image was not working, it made more sense to move on and keep production going.

## Source Verification

Most of my verification came from testing the tools myself.

I questioned whether the advice from videos actually worked by trying it inside my own project. If a prompting method helped keep characters consistent, I reused it. If it failed, I adjusted, reprompted, or used reference images.

For the music side, publishing links to Spotify, iTunes, and Apple Music can help verify that the AI-assisted music was not just a concept. It became an actual released product.

## How Research Shaped the Project

Research shaped the project by showing me that AI is not just a button that makes finished work. It needs structure.

The research helped me build a process around:

- saving reusable prompts,
- using reference images,
- checking outputs against the original idea,
- reprompting when things went wrong,
- deciding when to pivot,
- and knowing when I needed to take over.

The project became less about “Can AI make this for me?” and more about “How do I guide AI without losing control?”

## AI Tools and Prompt Experiments

The main tools used were:

- **ChatGPT** — organizer, prompt builder assistant, and tool recommender.
- **NotebookLM** — helped explain the process and show how someone else could follow it.
- **Figma Make** — used for comic book panels and page structure.
- **Firefly** — used for generating imagery.
- **Suno** — used to create music and make it publishable.

The most important AI experiments were:

- keeping comic characters consistent,
- keeping the music theme and style consistent,
- making the project feel intentional even though AI was involved,
- and figuring out how much AI should do versus when I should take over.

For the comic, consistency was the biggest challenge. I had to preserve prompts and reuse them so the characters did not drift away from their original style.

For the music, I gave ChatGPT lyrics, emotions, ideas, or interview answers. ChatGPT helped build lyrics and music direction, then I plugged that into Suno and kept generating until I heard something I liked.

The music style I eventually settled on was fast, aggressive thrash metal with tight riffs, double-kick drums, shouted vocals, melodic choruses, some ska/reggae/pop-punk rhythm influence, and a chaotic but controlled emotional tone.

## Process Page / Workflow and Pivots

The comic workflow usually looked like this:

1. Start with the story idea or scene.
2. Use ChatGPT to figure out what needed to happen next.
3. Build or revise the prompt.
4. Generate imagery in Firefly.
5. Check the image against the original idea.
6. Reprompt with references when the image was wrong.
7. Save useful prompts in notes.
8. Use Figma Make to organize the comic panels and pages.

The music workflow looked different:

1. Start with lyrics, emotions, or a song idea.
2. Use ChatGPT to help shape the lyrics and style direction.
3. Put the lyrics and style prompt into Suno.
4. Generate multiple versions.
5. Listen until one matched the intent.
6. Keep refining the style until it became consistent.
7. Use the finished songs for release.

A major pivot happened when the project expanded from comics into music. That showed the project was not only about AI-assisted comic-making. It became a larger project about AI-assisted entertainment production.

Another major pivot was learning that color made the comic workflow much harder. Black-and-white images were easier to control, but color introduced more consistency problems.

## Final Product or Outcome

By the end, I produced a sample comic with panels on pages.

I also produced three singles and an album that are now on iTunes and other music platforms.

The strongest part of the final outcome is that it shows AI can do a little or a lot, and the creator can still stay in control. The tradeoff is that it might take longer than expected.

## Product Page / Authorship and Synthesis

My authorship showed up in the choices I made.

For the comic, my judgment was mainly visual. Colors and consistency were extremely important. I had to decide whether the knights still looked right, whether the style was drifting, whether the color worked, and whether the image needed to be remade.

For the music, the judgment was more about matching the music style to the lyrics and adding a thing or two to make it stronger. After the second single, I decided on a style and stuck with it.

The human touch is not just adding fake mistakes so something looks less AI-made. The human touch is putting feeling, emotion, comparison, taste, and direction into the prompt and the build of what I am trying to achieve.

AI is a tool like a wrench, guitar, paintbrush, or anything else we use to build with.

## What I Learned

I learned that AI is not a tool that should be ignored, but it also should not be used lightly.

At the beginning, I thought AI should not be used because it felt like it might take or steal the idea. By the end, I saw it as a tool that takes practice, control, and responsibility.

I also learned that prompting is not only about being specific. It is also about knowing when to pivot. Sometimes it is better to readjust and move forward instead of wasting credits trying to fix one image.

The comic book-making side is where I had to grow the most. I had to adjust prompts when something was too violent or graphic, repeat wording to keep consistency, and keep correcting the AI until it understood what I was aiming for.

AI might have my kids beat at making me repeat myself.

## Reflection Notes

This project changed how I think about AI.

At first, I saw it as something that maybe should not be used. Now I see it as something that can be used, but not casually. It takes practice, patience, and human judgment.

The click moments happened twice:

1. When I figured out how to make the comic.
2. When I figured out how to make the music.

Both times, once I figured out how to do what I needed, I was gone.

The project helped me understand that AI-assisted work can still have intent and emotion if the person using it is actually steering it. It should not just be AI slop. It should have a reason, a direction, and a human behind it.
